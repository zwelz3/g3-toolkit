1 · The thesis

Every capture manager lives with two fears: bidding on work they can't win, and getting caught overclaiming when an evaluator probes their past performance. Today they manage both by memory and archaeology — digging across SharePoint, proposal archives, CRM exports, and Teams threads, re-doing the work for every pursuit, and trusting their gut on what's "really" relevant.

This demo shows a discipline they wish they had institutionalized: a graph that separates substantiated past performance from performative past performance, computes the bid decision on the substantiated floor, and surfaces precisely where the sellable story is exposed — then closes that exposure with a trusted teaming partner the graph already knows about.

The one-sentence pitch

“Your data, your rules — composed into a bid decision you can defend line by line, and a proposal story you know won't collapse under scrutiny.”

3 · The two-strength meaning model

The heart of the demo. When raw records project onto the concept layer, each capability/topic concept carries two derived strengths, not one:

Substantiated

What you can defend under scrutiny: a real award, backed by a strong customer evaluation (the dominant signal), documented in a final report, with traceable named delivery. Recency-weighted. This is the floor the bid decision runs on.

Claimable (performative)

What you could write in a proposal: everything substantiated, plus adjacent-concept work, unevaluated or marginal efforts, and self-asserted capability from people's résumés. The softer ceiling.

The exposure

On a required concept, the gap between claimable and substantiated is the firm's exposure: where the proposal would assert strength the evidence can't defend. That gap — not a keyword miss — is the thing the tool makes visible, and the thing every other tool is blind to because computing it requires the signed, weighted, provenance-bearing graph.

This yields three honest states per required concept:

State Substantiated Claimable What it means · the action
Discriminator High High Genuine strength, provable. Lead with it.
Exposed Low High You'd sell it; it won't survive scrutiny. Close it — team or named hire.
Gap Low Low Honest capability gap. Don't claim it; team for the capability.

Sustainment = Exposed. We'd claim it strongly (résumés, adjacent logistics work), but our provable evidence is thin — dragged down by that contested 2019 effort. If an evaluator probes, the story cracks. Cyber = Gap. We can't even credibly claim it. Two different problems, two different actions — a distinction a keyword tool literally cannot draw.

“The internal go/no-go can't run on what we'd write. It has to run on what we can defend.”
Act III — Decision → fan-out → the brief

Pursue — because the exposure is closeable.

7 · Decision on the substantiated floor deriveActions() on screen

A decision node forms: Pursue with teaming, justified by the substantiated 68% — not the rosier claimable number. It fans out (not 1:1) into citations, gap-teaming, discriminator-mapping, and customer-shaping actions, each tagged with provenance.

From the exposed/gap concepts, the graph traces outward and surfaces a partner Northwind has co-delivered substantiated, evaluated work with before — one who turns the cyber gap and the sustainment exposure into defensible claims. The exposure isn't fatal; it's closeable with someone already trusted. The decision solidifies.

5 · The deliverable — the two-faced capture brief

The artifact a capture director would pay for. Same evidence, two audiences:

Internal · the truth we bid on
  • Substantiated fit: 68% — pursue with teaming.
  • Provable discriminators: Digital Thread, Data Governance, Predictive Logistics — each backed by an Exceptional/Very-Good evaluation.
  • Exposed: Sustainment — we'd claim it; the evidence is thin (contested 2019 effort). Risk if probed.
  • Gap: Cyber Resilience — not credibly claimable.
  • Closeable: partner ORCA Systems — prior co-delivered, evaluated cyber + sustainment work.
Proposal · the story we'll defend
  • Lead Past Performance volume with HELIOS (Exceptional, recompete-won) and AEGIS (Exceptional, reused semantic layer).
  • Predictive logistics discriminator anchored by ATLAS (Very Good).
  • Sustainment & cyber carried by the ORCA teaming arrangement — claims now backstopped by real, evaluated joint past performance.
  • Section M mapping: each evaluation factor → the provable discriminator that answers it, within the 10-page limit.

Every line on both faces drills to its evidence — or, for the exposure, to the documented absence of it. That round-trip is the capability; the rest is presentation.

6 · Where the "wow" lives

Deliberately not one obvious bridge. The memorable moments are quiet and multi-signal — which is what makes them feel real rather than staged:

  • The two bars diverging. The instant a viewer sees substantiated fall away from claimable on Sustainment, they get it — and they recognize their own company in it.
  • One contract, two roles. The contested 2019 effort that weakens Sustainment is the same lesson that shaped the later ATLAS win — visible only because the graph keeps signed associations and provenance.
  • The teaming partner as resolution. The gap arrives with its own solution: a trusted partner the graph surfaces from prior co-delivery. The fear (we're exposed) resolves to confidence (we know how to close it) in one trace.

8 · The honesty line we hold

The demo is confident because it's honest about what's real:

  • The projection rule and the analytic are illustrative stand-ins — the demo signals they're yours to plug in, and never implies a specific proprietary method is "the answer." A BD audience that catches a faked algorithm distrusts everything; “here's where your logic goes, shown with a representative result” reads as correct.
  • What is genuinely the toolkit's, and what gets the spotlight, is the composition and the provenance: tiered transforms that stay traceable, end to end. That is not mocked.
  • One complete worked example runs all the way through, so it feels like a finished machine — with just-visible access panels where logic plugs in, not a box of parts.
